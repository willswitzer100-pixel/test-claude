Cell Biology

The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of all living organisms. Cells were first observed by Robert Hooke in 1665, and the cell theory — stating that all living things are composed of cells — became one of the foundational principles of biology.

Key Organelles

  • Nucleus — Contains the cell's DNA and controls gene expression.
  • Mitochondria — The powerhouse of the cell; generates ATP through cellular respiration.
  • Endoplasmic Reticulum — A network of membranes involved in protein and lipid synthesis.
  • Golgi Apparatus — Modifies, sorts, and packages proteins for transport.
  • Ribosomes — Molecular machines that synthesize proteins.
  • Cell Membrane — A selectively permeable barrier that controls what enters and exits the cell.

Prokaryotes vs. Eukaryotes

Prokaryotic cells (bacteria, archaea) lack a membrane-bound nucleus, while eukaryotic cells (plants, animals, fungi) contain a true nucleus and complex organelles. Despite these differences, all cells share fundamental processes like DNA replication, transcription, and translation.

Ecology

Ecology is the study of interactions among organisms and their environment. It encompasses everything from individual organisms to the biosphere as a whole.

Energy Flow

Energy flows through ecosystems in food chains and food webs. Producers (plants) convert sunlight into chemical energy via photosynthesis. Primary consumers (herbivores) eat producers, and secondary consumers (carnivores) eat herbivores. At each trophic level, roughly 90% of energy is lost as heat, which is why top predators are relatively rare.

Evolution

Evolution is the change in heritable characteristics of populations over successive generations. Charles Darwin and Alfred Russel Wallace independently proposed natural selection as the primary mechanism of evolution.

Mechanisms of Evolution

Natural Selection

Organisms with traits better suited to their environment are more likely to survive and reproduce. Over time, these advantageous traits become more common in the population.

Genetic Drift

Random changes in allele frequencies, especially significant in small populations. Events like bottlenecks and founder effects can drastically alter a population's genetic makeup.

Gene Flow

The transfer of genetic material between populations through migration. Gene flow tends to reduce differences between populations and increase genetic variation within them.

Mutation

Random changes in DNA sequence that introduce new alleles into a population. Mutations are the ultimate source of all genetic variation.

Evidence for Evolution

  • Fossil Record — Shows a progression of life forms over geological time.
  • Comparative Anatomy — Homologous structures reveal common ancestry (e.g., the forelimbs of mammals).
  • Molecular Biology — DNA and protein sequence comparisons confirm evolutionary relationships.
  • Biogeography — Geographic distribution of species reflects evolutionary history.
  • Direct Observation — Antibiotic resistance in bacteria and Darwin's finches show evolution in action.

Genetics

Genetics is the study of heredity and the variation of inherited characteristics. The field began with Gregor Mendel's experiments on pea plants in the 1860s and has since expanded to encompass molecular genetics, genomics, and epigenetics.

DNA Structure

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) is a double helix composed of two strands of nucleotides. Each nucleotide contains a sugar (deoxyribose), a phosphate group, and one of four nitrogenous bases: adenine (A), thymine (T), guanine (G), or cytosine (C). Base pairing rules dictate that A pairs with T and G pairs with C.

The Central Dogma

The central dogma of molecular biology describes the flow of genetic information:

DNA ⟶ Transcription ⟶ RNA ⟶ Translation ⟶ Protein

Mendelian Genetics

Mendel discovered that traits are determined by discrete units (now called genes) that come in pairs. His laws of segregation and independent assortment explain how alleles are separated during gamete formation and recombine during fertilization.
